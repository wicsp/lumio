Abstract—In recent times, the swift evolution of adversarial
attacks has captured widespread attention, particularly con-
cerning their transferability and other performance attributes.
These techniques are primarily executed at the sample level,
frequently overlooking the intrinsic parameters of models. Such
neglect suggests that the perturbations introduced in adversarial
samples might have the potential for further reduction. Given
the essence of adversarial attacks is to impair model integrity
with minimal noise on original samples, exploring avenues to
maximize the utility of such perturbations is imperative. Against
this backdrop, we have delved into the complexities of adversarial
attack algorithms, dissecting the adversarial process into two
critical phases: the Directional Supervision Process (DSP) and the
Directional Optimization Process (DOP). While DSP determines
the direction of updates based on the current samples and
model parameters, it has been observed that existing model
parameters may not always be conducive to adversarial attacks.
The impact of models on adversarial efficacy is often overlooked
in current research, leading to the neglect of DSP. We show
that, under certain conditions, temporarily fine-tuning model
parameters can improve the quality of the DSP. We provide
formal definitions and first-order conditions characterizing when
such updates increase the attack loss, and introduce several
practical methods for fine-tuning model parameters within DSP.
Our extensive experiments substantiate the effectiveness of the
proposed P3A method. Our code is available at an anonymous
repository: https://anonymous.4open.science/r/P3A-A12C/.
Index Terms—Adversarial attack, deep learning, robustness,
parameter adaptation, transferability
I. INTRODUCTION
Adversarial attacks are central to assessing and improving
the security of deep learning systems, especially when models
are deployed in safety- and mission-critical real-world appli-
cations. While recent work has largely shifted towardblack-
boxsettings for practical relevance,white-boxanalysis remains
invaluable: full access to model internals allows us to expose
subtle vulnerabilities and to develop stronger attacks that in
turn inform better defenses [1].
Most modern attacks improve effectiveness by refining
how perturbations areoptimized in input space. Representa-
tive examples include momentum-based [2], transformation-
based [3], and scale-invariant [4] variants. However, these
methods take the model parameters as fixed and treat the
gradient direction as given. We argue that the current param-
eter setting of a model may not be the mostattack-favorable
configuration for a given input: a slight, temporary adjustment
of parameters can yield a more informative gradient direction
and thus stronger perturbations.

To formalize this perspective, we conceptually split an
attack into two stages (Fig. 1): (i) aDirectional Supervi-
sion Process (DSP), which determines the update direction
for the perturbation (e.g., via∇ xL); and (ii) aDirectional
Optimization Process (DOP), which refines that direction
through momentum, transformations, frequency-domain tricks,
etc. Prior work predominantly innovates in DOP, while DSP is
implicitly accepted as whatever the fixed model provides. Our
key idea is toadapt the model parameters before (or during)
DSPto obtain a better direction.
Concretely, we introduceParameter Adaptive Adversarial
Attack (P3A), which performs lightweight, loss-guided fine-
tuning on alocal surrogate copyof the model to improve
the gradient direction used for crafting the input perturbation.
The final adversarial example is still evaluated on the original
(unchanged) model, which aligns with realistic transfer attack
settings. We show theoretically that small parameter updates
can increase the loss under mild conditions, and we provide
practical update rules that approximate the required second-
order effects efficiently.
Our contributions are as follows:
1) We highlight and formalize the often-overlooked DSP
stage of adversarial attacks and show that adapting
model parameters can improve this stage.
2) We derive conditions (via a directional finite-difference
view) under which small parameter updates increase
attack loss to first order, and we propose four practical
update strategies with intuitive motivations.
3) We empirically demonstrate that P3A consistently boosts
both white-box efficacy (loss/ASR growth per step) and

II. RELATEDWORK
A. White-box Attacks
White-box attacks assume full access to model parameters
and gradients and are widely used to evaluate robustness.
FGSM [5] applies a single gradient-sign step, while BIM [6]
iterates this update and PGD [7] further projects each step onto
anℓ p-bounded set, making it a standard robustness benchmark.
Optimization-based attacks such as C&W [8] formulate ad-
versarial generation as a constrained objective and often yield
highly imperceptible perturbations. These methods optimize
perturbations in input space with fixed model parameters,
and their effectiveness depends on the quality of the update
direction, connecting to our DSP discussion in Section III-C.
B. Black-box and Transfer Attacks
Black-box attacks often rely on transferability from surro-
gate models. Many methods enhance transfer by modifying
the perturbation optimization process (DOP) to stabilize or
diversify updates: MI-FGSM [2] introduces momentum, DI-
FGSM [3] uses randomized differentiable transformations, TI-
FGSM [9] enforces translation-invariance via gradient smooth-
ing, and SINI-FGSM [4] combines Nesterov acceleration with
scale-invariance. More recent approaches leverage integrated
or relevance-guided gradients for improved transfer, including
MIG [10] and GRA [11]. A complementary line modifies the
surrogate modelitself: SGM [12] reweights gradients through
skip connections, Ghost Networks [13] inject stochastic per-
turbations into skip connections to form an implicit ensemble,
and LGV [14] samples weights along a high-learning-rate tra-
jectory. These perturb parameterstask-agnosticallyand fixed
across inputs; P3A instead adaptsper input, keeps only updates
passing an explicit first-order criterion (Sec. III-E), and reverts
before evaluation on the original model. Overall, prior lines
refine DOP while treating the gradient direction as fixed; we
improve DSP by adapting parameters per input.
C. Adversarial Defenses
Adversarial training is a dominant defense strategy, com-
monly instantiated with PGD-generated examples [7]. Ensem-
ble defenses improve robustness by combining diverse models
or predictors to reduce the chance that a single perturbation
fools the system [15], [16]. RobustBench provides standard-
ized protocols for benchmarking robustness across models and
datasets [17]. Stronger attacks and reliable evaluation remain
essential for advancing defenses; our work contributes by
strengthening attacks under a practical compute budget.
From a data-mining standpoint, transferable attacks are a
practical tool forrobustness auditing: they stress-test deployed
models without access to their internals, which is the realistic
setting for security and privacy assessment of mined models.
A stronger transfer attack therefore yields a tighter, more
conservative robustness estimate, and a modular attack such
as ours—which plugs into existing pipelines and reverts every
parameter change—can be folded into standardized evaluation
suites without altering the model under test. We position P3A
in this light: not as a means to manipulate a remote system, but
as a transferable, reproducible probe of model vulnerability.
III. METHOD
A. Threat Model & Use Case
We assume awhite-box (or surrogate) setting: the attacker
has full access to alocal copyof the target modelf(·;θ),
including its architecture and gradients. During the attack
generation process, the attacker is allowed totemporarily fine-
tunethis local surrogate’s parameters to obtain more infor-
mative gradients (betterDirectional Supervision) for crafting
perturbations.
Crucially, the deployed target model remains unchanged.
The adversarial examplex adv produced with the help of
the adapted surrogate (parametersθ ′) isevaluated on the
original modelf(·;θ). This reflects realistic black-box transfer
scenarios where modifying the remote model is infeasible;
thus P3A primarily aims to boost transferability and to stress-
test model robustness rather than to assume direct parameter
manipulation of the victim.

B. Results
1) White-box Scenario:What is measured.All loss and
ASR values in this section are computed on theoriginal,
unadaptedmodelf(·;θ). Parameter adaptation is applied only
to a local surrogate copy to obtain the update direction
(DSP) and is reverted before the resulting perturbed input is
evaluated; we never report loss on the adapted parametersθ ′.
This is consistent with the threat model in Sec. III-A and
with the definition ofL θ′ in Theorem 2, whose outer loss
is evaluated atθ, and it avoids the circularity of measuring
loss on the very parameters that were tuned to increase it.
The reported gains therefore reflect a more informative update
direction, not a weakened model.
In Fig. 2, we document the incremental efficacy of P3A
with respect to cross-entropy loss across a suite of attacks
(BIM, PGD, DI-FGSM, TI-FGSM, MI-FGSM, SINI-FGSM);
the same trend holds for KL divergence. P3A augments both
metrics across all attack strategies. Versus the baselines, cross-
entropy loss rises by0.028on average and KL loss by0.031.
SINI-FGSM shows the largest boost (0.052CE /0.055KL)
and DI-FGSM the smallest (0.011both), indicating broad
compatibility of our approach.
As the attack iterations progress, we witness a diminish-
ing marginal increase in the Loss value. This trend can be
attributed to the decreasing sensitivity of the model to further
perturbations, reflective of the adaptive nature of deep learning
models under sustained adversarial pressure. Nevertheless, the
sustained incremental gains validate the potency and utility of
the P3A method in enhancing adversarial attacks step-by-step.
Further examination reveals that adversarially trained mod-
els, such as Inc-v3-adv and Inc-v3-adv-ens3, also benefit from
the P3A method, albeit to a lesser extent compared to models
without adversarial hardening like Inc-v3, Inc-v4, and Res-
50. This outcome suggests the efficacy of adversarial training
in bolstering model robustness, and concurrently, the P3A
method’s capacity to introduce improvements even within
robustified models.
Fig. 3 complements these findings by demonstrating the
incremental impact of the P3A method on the ASR across
attack steps for different models. The substantial increases in
ASR, particularly evident in adversarially trained models Inc-
v3-adv and Inc-v3-adv-ens3, underscore the adaptability and
enhanced performance of the P3A method in bolstering ASRs,
especially within models fortified against such adversarial
exploits.
2) Black-box Scenario:Table I presents a comparison of
the ASR between P3A and other state-of-the-art transferable
attack methods across various models. In all tested models,
P3A achieved the highest ASR in most cases, demonstrating
its superiority in transferable attacks. Notably, P3A’s perfor-
mance was particularly strong when using Inc-v3, Inc-v4, and
MaxViT-T as the source model, with average transfer ASRs
reaching 72.9%, 75.1%, and 77.3% respectively. This could
be attributed to P3A’s attack strategy effectively adapting to
and exploiting the specific vulnerabilities of these models.
Compared to the MIG and GRA methods, P3A exhibited
significant improvements against adversarially robust models
(e.g., Inc-v3-ens3 and Inc-v3-ens4). Even for more complex
Transformer architectures (ViT-B/16 and MaxViT-T), P3A also
demonstrated strong attack capabilities. The strongest source is
ResNet-152, where P3A attains the highest average in the table
(87.1%vs.74.8%for GRA and44.5%for MIG), with large
margins on hardened ensemble targets (IncRes-ens:80.0%vs.
54.4%); its rich skip-connection structure gives the adaptation
more freedom to reshape the input gradient.
Furthermore, to better position P3A against the most recent
advancements, we evaluate its transferability alongside ANDA
(CVPR 2024) and FoolMix (TIFS 2025). As summarized
in Table II, under the exact same evaluation protocol, P3A
consistently outperforms both methods across various architec-
tures, further corroborating its superiority in generating highly
transferable adversarial examples.
3) Computational Overhead:Since P3A evaluates multiple
candidate parameter-update directions via directional finite
difference, it requires additional gradient computations per
iteration. In Table III, we quantify this overhead by reporting
peak GPU memory and throughput (Frames Per Second, FPS)
under an identical hardware and software environment. P3A is
the most expensive method here—lowest throughput, highest
peak memory—because each iteration probes several signed
candidates with an extra gradient apiece. We state plainly when
this is worth it. The overhead suits the offline, high-value
regime that motivates transfer attacks (robustness auditing,
red-teaming), and pays off most on the hardest targets: against
adversarially trained and cross-architecture (CNN→ViT) tar-
gets P3A improves transfer ASR by10–25points over GRA
(Table I). When the source already transfers well and the
margin is small—e.g. a ViT-B/16 source, where P3A exceeds
GRA by only∼1.4points—a cheaper DOP-only attack may
be preferable. The candidate setFand probe count trade
accuracy for speed, so P3A is a strong-but-heavier option, not
a universal default. Concretely, Table III shows P3A at0.87
FPS and3312MB peak memory: it is faster than the relevance-
guided GRA (0.55FPS) yet slower than the lightweight MIG
(4.68FPS), with the memory cost reflecting the temporary
surrogate copy and the cached probe gradients. Because the
per-step cost grows only linearly in|F |(Sec. III-F) and not in
the input size, the overhead stays within a practical range for
offline adversarial-example generation, and shrinking|F |from
four candidates to the singleDefensiverule recovers most of
the gain at a fraction of the cost.

directions without overshooting.
d) Take-away.:Among the factors studied, theDefensive

followed by a learning rate of10 −4 and a modest step size
α= 1. These settings form the default configuration for P3A
in all subsequent experiments.
Notably, we observe that P3A yields the most significant
improvements on adversarially trained models and in cross-
architecture transfer scenarios (e.g., CNNViT), suggesting
that our method is particularly effective in challenging attack
settings where conventional approaches tend to struggle.
e) Why adapting parameters helps DSP .:The criterion
of Theorems 2–3 keeps a parameter update only when the
induced change in the input gradient,g ′
θ −g θ, is positively
correlated with the current directiong θ. Intuitively, this selects
parameter perturbations thatsharpenthe loss landscape along
the very direction the attack is already exploiting, rather than
rotating it arbitrarily. Two observations make this concrete.
First, the gain is largest precisely where the fixed-parameter
gradient is least reliable: against adversarially trained and
ensemble-defended targets, whose decision boundaries are
deliberately flattened around clean inputs, a small surrogate
adaptation recovers a steeper, more informative ascent di-
rection, which is consistent with the10–25point margins
we observe there. Second, in cross-architecture transfer the
surrogate gradient is biased toward source-specific features;
theDefensive Perspectiveupdate—gradient ascent on the loss
with respect toθ—pushes the surrogate toward parameters that
emphasize broadly shared, transfer-friendly features, which
helps explain its consistent edge over the gradient-freeUni-
formandMagnituderules in Fig. 4. Crucially, because the
adapted surrogate is reverted and the adversarial example is
evaluated on the original model, these benefits manifest as a
better updatedirectionrather than as an artifact of a weakened
evaluation model. Together with the per-iteration selection, this
explains why P3A improves a range of base attacks (Figs. 2, 3)
without method-specific tuning.
D. Limitations
We note several limitations that delineate the scope of our
claims and motivate future work.(i) Perturbation norm.Our
experiments use the standardℓ ∞ budget; while the DSP/DOP
decomposition and the DFD criterion are norm-agnostic, the
interaction between parameter adaptation and other constraints
(e.g.ℓ 2) and the resulting perceptual quality of the adversarial
examples remain to be studied.(ii) Task and modality.We eval-
uate on ImageNet classification; extending P3A to detection,
segmentation, regression, or to non-image modalities such as
text and tabular data is left open, although the formulation
only assumes a differentiable loss.(iii) Compute.As analyzed
above, P3A multiplies the per-step gradient cost by1 + 2|F |;
for sources that already transfer well the extra cost may
outweigh the marginal gain, so the candidate set should be
sized to the compute budget.(iv) Order of approximation.The
selection criterion is first-order (Taylor plus DFD); higher-
order corrections could yield a tighter rule but at additional
cost.

V. CONCLUSION
In conclusion, this work presents a comprehensive examina-
tion of adversarial attacks through the lens of the Directional
Supervision Process (DSP) and the Directional Optimization
Process (DOP), delineating a nuanced approach to enhancing
the efficacy of such attacks via Parameter Adaptive adver-
sarial attack (P3A) methodology. By identifying the role of
parameter fine-tuning and introducing four parameter-update
strategies, we demonstrate consistent improvements in adver-
sarial attack strategies. Our experimental validation indicates
the robustness and versatility of our approach across various
models and attack vectors. This work focuses on non-targeted
attacks, which aim to induce any incorrect model prediction;
extending P3A to targeted attack settings is left for future
work. Looking forward, we aim to explore the implications
of extending the parameter exploration to more iterations and
incorporating higher-order properties in the DSP, potentially
unveiling deeper insights into model vulnerabilities and ad-
vancing the state of adversarial attack methodologies.
