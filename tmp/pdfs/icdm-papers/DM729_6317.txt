AbstractвҖ”Although recent advances in Large Language Mod-
els (LLMs) have significantly improved their capability to gen-
erate coherent and context-aware responses, these LLMs remain
vulnerable to interactions that can bypass safety alignment
mechanisms, making the identification of such vulnerabilities
essential in AI safety. Existing jailbreak approaches, includ-
ing single-turn, multi-turn, and search-based methods, have
demonstrated improved attack effectiveness by leveraging crafted
instructions, conversational context, and optimization strategies.
However, single-turn methods are not effective to modern LLMs
with stronger safety alignment, multi-turn methods rely on
heuristic interaction patterns, and search-based methods do
not consider explicitly model structured trajectories evolving
over multiple steps over a sequence of interactions. There are
few works to capture how a sequence of semantically benign
queries can collectively reconstruct a high-risk objective for
effective LLM jailbreaking attack. To address these limitation,
we propose K-GATE, a novel framework that formulates LLM
jailbreak as an adversarial trajectory mining problem over a
knowledge-decomposed graph. The central hypothesis is that
safety alignment exhibits non-monotonic safety landscape: while
individual queries may remain safe, their composition can
induce safety policy violation. K-GATE constructs knowledge-
decomposed graphs, conducts Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS)-
based trajectory mining, and trajectory amplification via sequen-
tial context synthesis. Experimental results on two representa-
tive LLM safety benchmarks show that K-GATE achieves the
highest attack success rate, outperforming eight state-of-the-art
jailbreaking methods by significant margins, while maintaining
strong stealthiness under safety guardrails. These findings suggest
that current LLM safety alignment mechanisms are locally robust
but vulnerable under sequential interaction trajectories.

I. INTRODUCTION
Recent advances in Large Language Models (LLMs) have
significantly improved their capability to generate coherent and
context-aware responses [1], [2]. However, since these LLMs
remain vulnerable to interactions and conversations that can
bypass safety alignment mechanisms, raising critical concerns
in AI safety [3], [4]. As LLMs are increasingly deployed
in real-world applications, identifying these vulnerabilities
has become an essential research [5], [6]. Especially, LLM
jailbreak attack has emerged as a key direction for assessing
the LLM safety alignment, aiming to expose failure modes
before they can be used in practice [4], [7], [8].
Existing jailbreak approaches are broadly categorized into
three directions. First, single-turn jailbreak attacks focus on
crafting a single prompt that embeds malicious intent within
carefully designed templates or instructions [5], [6], [9], [10].
Second, multi-turn jailbreak attacks exploit the conversational
context by gradually steering the LLM through a sequence
of interactions [7], [8], [11]. Third, search-based approaches
formulate jailbreak as an optimization problem and employ
search algorithms to identify effective attack strategies [12],
[13]. These approaches have demonstrated that leveraging
crafted instructions, context, and search can improve attack
effectiveness compared to simple prompt-level injections.

or manually designed interaction patterns, lacking systematic
mechanisms to explore the space of possible interactions.
Current search-based methods improve automation, but fail to
explicitly model structured trajectories evolving over multiple
steps over a sequence of interactions. Especially, there are
few works to capture how a sequence of semantically be-

II. RELATED WORK
A. Single-turn Jailbreaking Attack
Early LLM jailbreaking works predominantly operate in a
single-turn setting, where adversaries craft a single prompt
to bypass safety alignment [5], [6], [9], [10]. These methods
focus on prompt engineering techniques that embed malicious
intent within carefully designed templates or instructions. For
example,DeepInception[9] leverages nested and fictional con-
texts to disguise harmful queries.PAIR[6] iteratively refines
prompts using LLM feedback to improve attack effectiveness.
PAP[5] proposes persuasive adversarial prompts that exploit
the LLMвҖҷs compliance tendencies.GPTFuzzer[10] generates
jailbreak prompts inspired by fuzzing techniques.
Despite their effectiveness, single-turn methods are inher-
ently limited by their static nature. They treat jailbreak as
an isolated prompt-level problem, failing to utilize the se-
quential and contextual dynamics of multi-turn interactions.
Furthermore, although slight variations of a prompt can induce
moderate attack success, it has been observed that recent state-
of-the-art LLMs gradually become robust to such single-turn
attacks.
B. Multi-turn Jailbreaking Attack
To overcome the limitations of single-turn attacks, several
works explore multi-turn jailbreak attacks that exploit the
conversational context [7], [8], [11]. These methods gradually
manipulate the LLM over multiple interactions, leveraging the
accumulation of context to bypass LLM safety alignments.
For example,Foot-in-the-Door (FITD)[7] applies incremen-
tal persuasion techniques, starting from benign queries and
progressively steering the LLM toward harmful outputs. Be-
yond manually designed prompts,ActorAttack[8] proposes
role-playing strategies that induce LLM to adopt alternative
personas, thereby weakening its safety alignments. Similarly,
Crescendo[11] orchestrates multi-step conversational escala-
tion to break LLM safety alignments.
While multi-turn attacks demonstrate stronger effectiveness
by leveraging the conversational context, they rely on heuristic
or manually designed interaction patterns. Thus, they lack
systematic mechanisms for exploring the space of possible
interaction paths. This limitation prevents them from fully
capturing the structured nature of the jailbreaking mechanism.
C. Search-based Jailbreaking Attack
Recently, another line of research has formulated jailbreak
as an optimization search problem [12], [13]. These methods
typically employ search algorithms or heuristic exploration
to identify high-performing jailbreak strategies. Representa-
tively,KDA[13] exploits positional vulnerabilities in LLMs
by searching for effective prompt structures that maximize
attack success. Search-based methods have significantly im-
proved scalability and automation compared to the existing
approaches.
However, there are few works that explicitly capture and
model jailbreak as a structured trajectory evolving over multi-
ple steps over a sequence of interactions. This gap motivates
our approach, K-GATE, which frames jailbreak as a trajectory
mining problem and systematically explores the space of
multi-step interactions.

IV. PROPOSEDMETHOD: K-GATE
The pipeline of the K-GATE begins with knowledge discov-
ery via decomposition of a harmful goal into a directed acyclic
graph (DAG), followed by the trajectory mining via Monte
Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) [15], [16] and adaptive mutation
strategies to generate stealth attack prompts. Parts of the LLM
responses obtained from the target LLM are finally aggregated
to construct a potentially harmful and stealthy prompt through
sequential context synthesis.
A. Framework Overview
The core philosophy of K-GATE is that LLM safety align-
ments exhibit anon-monotonic safety landscape. While
LLMs are robust against single-turn harmful queries through
LLM safety alignments, it is observed that they are vulnerable
to multiple interactions. Furthermore, we assume jailbreaking
capability from the interactions is more effective when a high-
level harmful goalGis decomposed into seemingly benign
units of knowledge without revealing attackersвҖҷ malicious
intents. The operational flow is structured into a three-stage
pipeline as summarized in Figure 2.

A. Experimental Setup
Datasets.To evaluate the effectiveness of K-GATE, we
utilize two representative LLM safety benchmarks:Harm-
Bench[14] andAdvBench[3]. They provide a diverse set
of harmful prompts across various categories 8, enabling a
comprehensive assessment of LLM safety. From these bench-
marking datasets, we extract high-level harmful goals (G)
and decompose them into knowledge-based DAGs. Table I
summarizes the statistics of the two benchmarks.
Evaluation Metrics.We adopt theAttack Success Rate
(ASR)[7], [11], defined as the ratio of interaction trajec-
tories that successfully elicit harmful information from the
target LLM. Following existing works [8], [13], we employ
theLLM-as-a-Judgeto evaluate whether target LLMs are
jailbreaked or not by analyzing the LLM input and output.
To optimize inference cost, we implement a two-stage evalu-
ation process: a regex-based refusal filter serves as an initial
screening step to identify obvious refusals and the remaining
ambiguous cases are forwarded to the LLM-as-a-judge for
final scoring. The values are averaged across 100 repeated

B. Overall Performance
Tables II and III present the ASR of K-GATE and the
baseline methods on HarmBench and AdvBench, respec-
tively. Overall, K-GATE consistently achieves the highest ASR
across all evaluated LLMs, demonstrating the effectiveness
of adversarial trajectory mining. On HarmBench, K-GATE
achieves an average ASR of86.2%, significantly outperform-
ing the strongest baseline, KDA (73.6%), by+12.6 percent-
age points. Similarly, on AdvBench, K-GATE achieves an
average ASR of78.3%, outperforming the strongest baseline,
KDA (64.9%), by+13.4 percentage points. These results
confirm that mining adversarial trajectories over knowledge-
decomposed graphs is significantly more effective than exist-
ing prompt-level and search-based methods.
We also observe a clear disparity between closed-source
and open-weight LLMs. While state-of-the-art closed-source
LLMs exhibit relatively strong robustness with lower ASR
values, open-weight LLMs remain highly vulnerable. Espe-
cially, K-GATE achieves near-saturation performance on open-
weight LLMs, with ASR exceeding90%across all cases
(e.g., up to 99.7% on HarmBench), indicating that existing
safety alignment mechanisms in open-weight LLMs are still
insufficient against existing jailbreaking methods.
Another empirical finding is that single-turn attacks have
become significantly less effective against state-of-the-art and
modern LLMs. Unlike earlier studies [5], [6], [9], where
single-turn jailbreak prompts could achieve moderate ASRs,
our results show that single-turn methods (e.g., PAP, DeepIn-
ception) yield near-zero ASR on recent closed-source LLMs.
This trend suggests that advances in safety alignment train-
ing have strengthened defenses against single-turn attacks.

VI. ETHICALCONSIDERATIONS
The research of K-GATE, while demonstrating ASR, is
driven by a defensive philosophy aimed at identifying align-
ment vulnerabilities in LLMs. We adhere to the following
ethical principles: (1) the primary objective of our work
provide red-teaming experts with a jailbreaking framework to
assess LLM robustness more effectively than manual prompt
engineering; (2) our analysis of guardrails highlights the neces-
sity of stateful, history-aware defense mechanisms rather than
simple turn-level filtering; (3) All experiments were conducted
using established safety benchmarks, such as HarmBench
and AdvBench, ensuring that the evaluation is grounded in
recognized research standards for AI safety and alignment;
(4) We acknowledge the dual-use nature of this work. While
the implementation details are provided for reproducibility,
potentially harmful attack templates and trajectory-generation
prompts will only be shared with verified security researchers
and red-teaming practitioners after publication.
VII. CONCLUSION
Based on the hypothesis that LLM safety alignment exhibits
non-monotonicity across interaction trajectories, K-GATE per-
forms knowledge decomposition, MCTS-based trajectory min-
ing, and trajectory amplification via sequential context syn-
thesis. We formulate LLM jailbreaking as an adversarial
trajectory mining problem and empirically demonstrate the
super-additive risk phenomenon where individually benign
queries can collectively induce safety policy violation. For
future work, we plan to investigate more robust and con-
trollable knowledge decomposition strategies, develop more
efficient trajectory search methods, and extend our framework
toward trajectory-aware defense mechanisms that can mitigate
sequential interaction vulnerabilities in LLMs.
