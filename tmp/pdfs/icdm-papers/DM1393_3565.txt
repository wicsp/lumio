Abstract—Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) have
shown strong capabilities in visual understanding, reasoning,
and instruct following. However, their safety alignment remains
fragile when facing complex multimodal inputs and cross-modal
reasoning. While significant progress has been made in text-based
safety alignment for LLMs, MLLMs frequently produce unsafe
responses in context-dependent scenarios that require reasoning
over both the visual scene and the text inputs. Existing inference-
time defenses either steer generation with safety prefixes or
transform images into textual descriptions, remain deficient
in addressing context-dependent multimodal safety risks. To
enhance the safety of MLLMs, we propose SAGE (Safety-
Aware Grounding with External-Knowledge Enhancement), a
novel training-free alignment method consisting of two key
components: first, it elicits the model’s intrinsic safety reasoning
via a structured, multi-step prompting strategy; second, it com-
pensates for internal knowledge gaps by leveraging a curated
external safety knowledge base. Extensive experiments on public
safety benchmarks demonstrate that SAGE substantially reduces
unsafe responses across multiple backbone models. Notably, it
reduces the Unsafe Response Rate (USR) by up to 55.33%
on MSSBench compared with vanilla InternVL3.5-14B, while
maintaining general utility and improving ScienceQA accuracy
from 81.86% to 84.31%. The code and safety knowledge base
will be publicly released upon acceptance.

I. INTRODUCTION
Multimodal Large Language Models have recently achieved
remarkable progress in vision-language understanding and rea-
soning. Typically, MLLMs integrate a vision encoder, such as
ViT, with a large language model through a cross-modal pro-
jection or fusion module [1, 2]. The vision encoder transforms
input images into visual features, which are then mapped into

the textual embedding space and processed by the LLM to
generate responses. Benefiting from this architecture, MLLMs
have shown strong performance on various multimodal tasks,
including visual question answering (VQA) [3] and image-text
understanding [4].
Despite these capabilities, MLLMs face serious safety
challenges. While significant efforts have been devoted to
the textual safety alignment of LLMs (e.g., RLHF, SFT)[5]
and enhancing MLLMs’ general instruction-following abilities
[6], research on safety alignment specifically for multimodal
scenarios remains relatively scarce. Existing multimodal safety
datasets are limited in both scale and coverage, hindering the
safety alignment and deployment of MLLMs in complex real-
world scenarios.
Recently, several training-free methods [7, 8, 9] have been
proposed to improve the MLLM safety at inference time.
However, these approaches suffer from two key limitations.
First, simple interventions like safety prefixes often result in
only shallow activation of the model’s reasoning capability,
proving insufficient for handling implicit malicious intents
[10, 7]. Second, they rely heavily on the model’s intrinsic
safety knowledge, which is often incomplete due to the limited
coverage and granularity of current safety training data [11].
This gap is exacerbated in multimodal contexts where even
individually safe input modalities can combine to produce
harmful outputs, a problem requiring robust, long-chain rea-
soning [12, 13].
To address these limitations, we propose SAGE, a novel
training-free safety alignment method that enhances MLLM
safety from two complementary perspectives. First, we design
a targeted, multi-step prompting strategy that guides the model
through image description, query rewriting, and intent analy-
sis, thereby more fully unlocking its intrinsic safety reasoning

capabilities. Second, we construct an external knowledge base
that provides structured safety knowledge during inference.
This allows the model to perform reflection and generation
grounded in explicit safety rules, compensating for deficiencies
in its internal safety awareness.
The main contributions of our work are summarized as
follows:
•We design a multi-step risk reasoning strategy that guides
MLLMs to perform image description, query rewriting
and intent analysis, enabling more fine-grained under-

•We construct an external safety knowledge base to pro-

the model to reflect on potential risks and generate safer
responses.
•We conduct extensive experiments on multiple multi-

multimodal capabilities.
II. RELATEDWORK
Fine-tuning-based alignment.Conventional alignment of
MLLMs with human preferences relies on fine-tuning-based
strategies, such as RLHF[5] or supervised fine-tuning on
curated datasets[14]. Some methods also use architectural
modifications, e.g., inserting pre-trained classifiers to assess
unsafe content[8].
These training-based approaches share systemic limitations:
they are resource-intensive, may undermine general MLLM
capabilities through imbalanced training data, and remain
domain-specific and thus are vulnerable to novel attacks and
adversarial inputs.
Training-free Safety Alignment.Recent studies explore
training-free methods that directly intervene during inference.
Gou et al. propose ECSO[7], based on the observation that
the safety mechanism of pre-aligned LLMs is suppressed by
image features. ECSO detects harmful content in the initial
response, converts the image to text via query-aware image-to-
text transformation, and regenerates the response solely from
the resulting text by activating the intrinsic safety mechanism
of LLMs.
Ding et al. propose ETA[8], grounded in the insight that
continuous visual embeddings cause safety failures. ETA
builds a two-stage “Evaluation-Alignment” framework: it com-
bines CLIP-based pre-generation and reward model-based
post-generation evaluations, then employs a dual-level align-
ment consisting of shallow safety prefix as well as sentence-
level best-of-N search to guide safe and useful generation.
Zhu et al. propose GuardAlign[9], which uses optimal trans-
port to detect unsafe visual regions by measuring distances
between image patches and unsafe prompts, then masks those
regions. It also designs a cross-modal attention calibration to
reinforce safety prefix tokens throughout decoding, ensuring
consistent safety signals.
While existing training-free methods focus on input trans-
formation (ECSO, GuardAlign) or output guidance (ETA,
GuardAlign), our SAGE enhances the core reasoning process
itself. By structuring the model’s ”thought process” and aug-
menting it with external knowledge, we enable a deeper and
more robust form of safety alignment.
III. METHODOLOGY
In this section, we propose SAGE, a safety-knowledge-
enhanced prompt-based alignment method to improve the safe
generation in MLLMs at inference time. SAGE is training-
free and relies on prompt engineering and an external safety
knowledge base, rather than parameter updates, to guide step-
by-step safety reasoning. As shown in Fig. 1, the overall
workflow consists of three sequential stages.
A. Grounded Behavior Summary and Intent Classification
MLLMs often show asymmetric safety awareness across
modalities. While the underlying LLM has extensive text-
based safety training, it is less sensitive to visual content,
which can be exploited to bypass safety checks. Previous work
[7] attempted to convert images into textual descriptions and
directly apply text-based safe generation; however, it fails to
elicit fine-grained safety reasoning for the cases where both
input image and textual query are safe and the hazard is
implicit in their interaction.
To address this limitation, we design a chain-of-thought-
style prompt to guide the model to construct a unified,
semantically rich scene representation and infer the user’s true
intention. This is particularly important in multimodal scenar-
ios, where unsafe intent may be distributed across both visual
content and textual instructions. Prior work on multimodal
chain-of-thought also suggests that separating intermediate
rationale generation from final prediction can help models
better use multimodal information [15].

B. Knowledge Base Construction, Retrieval, and Matching
After image description and question rewriting, MLLMs
may lack specific security knowledge when dealing with
security-sensitive scenarios, potentially generating harmful
content. This inspires us to construct a safety knowledge
base and feed it into the model as prompts to support safety
reasoning.
Knowledge Base Construction.After analyzing frequently
encountered safety issues in existing datasets, we manually
construct a safety knowledge base containing 9 unsafe cat-
egories and 93 corresponding safety rules. Since different
datasets often use inconsistent safety labels and different
levels of granularity, we first map original risk labels into
a unified category taxonomy. Specifically, similar labels are
merged into the following nine unsafe categories: Self-Harm
and Suicide, Dangerous Behavior, Immorality Behavior, Illegal
Activity, Controversial Topic, Discrimination and Unequal
Treatment, Religion Beliefs, Information Misinterpretation,
and Privacy Violation. Detailed examples of the safety rules in
the knowledge base are presented in Appendix A, using Self-
Harm and Suicide and Information Misinterpretation as two
representative categories. The Safe category is treated as the
default state when none of the unsafe categories is triggered.
This unified taxonomy enables data from different sources
to be fused within the same semantic space and reduces
inconsistencies caused by label naming differences, granularity
mismatch, and ambiguous category boundaries. The detailed
category partition and statistical distribution of the rules in the
established knowledge base are summarized in Table I.
During construction, we do not restrict knowledge entries
to a single ”action-consequence” format. Instead, each entry is
defined for a general risk situation. This design is motivated by
the fact that risks in multimodal safety question answering do
not always appear as explicit dangerous operations. Some risks
originate from the user’s intention or expression, some from
contextual constraints in the image, and others from legal rules,
religious or cultural taboos, or privacy boundaries. Therefore,

dangerous or implicit intentions in user requests; (2) action–
scene–consequence risk knowledge, which describes harmful
outcomes of specific behaviors in particular environments;
and (3) norm-constraint risk knowledge, which captures non-
physical risks related to public order, legal rules, academic
integrity, and religious culture.

trollable without additional training.
Knowledge Matching.After retrieving category-specific
safety knowledge, directly generating the final response may
still be insufficient, because the model may fail to associate the
retrieved rules with the concrete user behavior. Therefore, we
introduce an explicit knowledge matching step to bridge the
grounded behavior and the retrieved safety knowledge. This
step selects the rule most applicable to the current request
and presents it as an explicit safety signal before response
generation, helping the model infer potential risks and activate
its internal safety mechanism.

IV. EXPERIMENTS
In this section, we conduct comprehensive empirical ex-
periments to verify the efficacy of SAGE. We focus on three
research questions: (1) Compared with existing baseline meth-
ods, whether it can effectively enhance the safety performance
of MLLMs against various risky multimodal queries? (2) Can
it preserve the general utility of MLLMs on benign tasks? (3)
How much does each key component, including the structured
safety prompt for image description and question rewriting,
and the external safety knowledge base, contribute to the
overall safety improvement?
A. Experimental Setup
Datasets.We evaluate safety performance on several
public multimodal safety benchmarks, including two types
of datasets: (1) Jailbreak attack datasets, including MM-
SafetyBench[18] and SPA-VL (Harm subset)[14]; (2) Safety-
awareness datasets, including MSSBench[19] and SIUO[20].
MSSBench and SIUO are employed to assess the model’s
capability in perceiving implicit safety issues, while MM-
SafetyBench and SPA-VL (Harm) focus on evaluating model
robustness under adversarial attack scenarios. Detailed de-
scriptions of each dataset are provided in the Appendix C1.
Meanwhile, to evaluate the general capabilities of SAGE, we
employ a series of widely used visual question answering and
multimodal reasoning benchmarks, including ScienceQA[21],
TextVQA[22] and MME[23]. More details about the bench-
mark settings and evaluation protocols are provided in Ap-
pendix C2.
Baseline Models.We select several representative open-
source MLLMs as backbone models for evaluation, in-
cluding Qwen3-VL-4B-Instruct, Qwen3-VL-8B-Instruct[24],
InternVL3.5-4B, InternVL3.5-8B, and InternVL3.5-14B[25].
We conduct all experiments on an NVIDIA RTX 5090 GPU.
During inferring, we setdo sample=F alseto ensure
deterministic outputs for reproducibility.
Comparison Methods.To comprehensively validate the ef-
fectiveness of our proposed method, we compare the following
approaches on each backbone model: (1)Direct Prompt-
ingbaseline, where the backbone model generates responses
without any defense mechanism; (2)ECSO, an inference-
time defense method that activates the intrinsic safety mech-
anisms of LLMs via image-to-text transformation; (3)ETA,
an inference-time safety alignment framework that combines
multimodal evaluation with bi-level alignment.
B. Performance On Safety
To assess the ability of SAGE to generate safe content, we
compare it with two existing safety enhancement strategies,
ECSO and ETA, across five representative multimodal models.
Specifically, ECSO is evaluated on all five models, while ETA
is evaluated on three models, excluding Qwen3 series, due to
the substantial model-specific reproduction effort required for
ETA.
Evaluation Metrics.We adopt the Unsafe Response Rate
(USR) as the primary metric for safety evaluation. USR is
defined as the proportion of unsafe responses generated by
the model among all responses, i.e.:
USR= Nunsafe
Ntotal
whereN unsafe denotes the total number of responses classified
as unsafe, andN total is the total number of test cases. A lower
USR indicates stronger model safety.
Evaluation Method.To determine whether the model-
generated response is safe, we employ Qwen3.6-27B-
Uncensored-HauhauCS-Balanced as a safety judge model. For
each instance, the model receives the user query, a correspond-
ing safety warning describing potential risks, the reference
answer, and the model output. The judge is prompted to reason
step by step in combination with these given information
and output a structured judgement indicating whether the
generated response is safe, along with a textual explanation
of its reasoning. The detailed prompts for safety evaluation
are presented in the Appendix D.
SAGE provides effective and robust safety enhancement
for MLLMs.Table II reports the USR of SAGE compared
with two safety enhancement methods, ECSO and ETA, across
multiple backbone models and four safety benchmarks. Vanilla
MLLMs exhibit non-negligible safety risks under multimodal
safety evaluation, particularly on SIUO and MSSBench, reach-
ing around 60–80% USR on InternVL3.5. Existing defenses
such as ECSO and ETA provide moderate improvements in
some cases. In contrast, SAGE consistently reduces USR

its effectiveness against both implicit cross-modal risks and
explicitly harmful multimodal inputs.

D. Ablation Study
We conduct ablation experiments on the SIUO benchmark
using Qwen3-VL-4B and InternVL3.5-14B to analyze the
contribution of each component. In Table IV, w/o KB denotes
the variant that keeps the safety-aware grounding module, in-
cluding image description and question rewriting, but removes
the external safety knowledge base, while w/ KB denotes the
full setting. The results show that both the multimodal intent-
analysis module (image description and question rewriting)
and the external safety knowledge base contribute to the final
safety improvement. Specifically, the image description and
question rewriting module plays a major role, by grounding
the user query in the visual context and enabling the model
to identify potential risks from multimodal inputs before
generation. On top of this safety-aware representation, the
external safety knowledge base further provides explicit and
structured safety constraints, which help the model make more
reliable judgments and suppress residual unsafe responses.
Although the improvement brought by the knowledge base
is moderate compared with the front-end intent analysis mod-
ule, removing it consistently leads to higher USR, indicating
that the knowledge base provides additional safety gains. This
suggests that the safety knowledge base plays a complemen-
tary and beneficial role by supplying external safety evidence
for complex or ambiguous cases.
E. Inference Efficiency Analysis
We further analyze the inference efficiency of SAGE. Since
training-free safety alignment methods usually introduce ad-
ditional inference-time operations, it is important to evaluate
whether the improved safety performance comes with ex-
cessive computational overhead. Therefore, we compare the
average inference time per sample between ECSO and SAGE.
ECSO performs safety enhancement by first detecting unsafe
responses and then regenerating answers based on image-to-
text transformation. In contrast, SAGE introduces a multi-
stage reasoning process, including image description, question
rewriting, intention classification, knowledge retrieval, knowl-
edge matching, and safety-aware response generation. When
only the front-end intent analysis module is used and the
knowledge base is removed from generation, which still yields
good results as shown in Table IV for the case ”w/o KB”, the
average inference time of our method is about 5.0 s, similar
to that of ECSO. The inference time is around 10 20 s for the

V. CONCLUSIONS
In this paper, we propose SAGE, a novel training-free
framework for enhancing the safety alignment of multimodal
large language models. SAGE leverages step-by-step reasoning
and external safety knowledge enhancement to address the
challenges of cross-modal safety issues. By integrating a struc-
tured safety-aware prompt and a manually constructed compre-
hensive safety knowledge base, SAGE guides MLLMs through
grounded behavior summarization, intent classification, knowl-
edge retrieval and rule matching to foster knowledge-grounded
reflection and ultimately produce safer responses. Our exten-
sive experiments on various safety benchmarks demonstrate
that SAGE significantly reduces the Unsafe Response Rate
across different MLLM backbones, outperforming existing
defense strategies by a large margin. Crucially, SAGE achieves
this robust safety improvement while largely preserving the
models’ general utility and generative capabilities.
In the future, we will focus on two key directions: first, ex-
panding and dynamically updating the safety knowledge base,
potentially through automated knowledge mining from real-
world interactions; second, designing more efficient knowl-
edge retrieval mechanisms to reduce latency.

A. Safety Knowledge Base
The knowledge base we constructed contains 93 safety rules
organized into nine unsafe categories. We use it for knowl-
edge retrieval, rule matching, and consequence reflection. The
specific entries for Self-Harm and Suicide and Information
Misinterpretation are as follows:
Self-Harm and Suicide
SH1: Express intention to end life (e.g., saying ”want to leave to visit the
deceased people”).
SH2: Seek methods, instructions, or specific ways to commit self-harm or
suicide (e.g., hang with a rope, try to drown).
SH3: Engage in or depict ongoing self-harm behavior (e.g., cutting, burning,
head bang or other forms of self-injury).
SH4: Indirect or implicit expressions of hopelessness or self-harm ideation
(e.g., ”life is meaningless”, ”hate my life”).
SH5: Participate in or promote self-harm related challenges, communities, or
harmful trends.
SH6: Glorify or conceal self-harm or suicide behavior in other ways (e.g.,
want to know the blood flow status under the skin).
SH7: Any behavior that may directly or indirectly lead to physical harm to
oneself or risk of death (e.g., excessive drinking).
Information Misinterpretation
IM1: Accidental ingestion of dangerous foods (e.g., eat wild mushrooms that
may be poisonous etc.).
IM2: Facilitate relationships between individuals inappropriately (e.g., set up
a romantic relationship between a woman and a young boy etc.).
IM3: Use non-monetary forms of banknote to shop.
IM4: Training animal actors is against animal protection regulations, it is
inappropriate for the user to use animals for performance.

B. Implementation Detalis
1) Prompts for the Grounded Behavior Summary and Intent
Classification:The following prompt templates, including
Image Description, Behavior Summary and Intention Analysis,
collectively constitute the CAT TEMPLATE used in the first
stage of our safety enhancement pipeline.
1.P desc: Image Description
You are given:
[Question]:{question}First, clearly identify and describe all key visual
elements in the image, including: subjects (people, objects, architecture),
position (e.g., rooftop), environment (e.g., in quiet library) and texts.
IMPORTANT: Based on the Image Description, determine whether the
visible scene itself contains a high-risk environment, hazardous location,
active danger, exposed infrastructure, or unsafe condition (e.g., train tracks,
not wearing seat belt).
Output exactly: Scene Hazard or Constraint: Yes / No
2.P rewrite: Behavior Summary
Combine the Image Description with the user’s full request to analyze:
- WHAT the user intends to do: the action, task, question, or behavior
expressed in the user’s request.
- WHERE this behavior would occur: the visible scene/environment/location
in the image.
Step 1: Determine the user’s intent type:
- Observation/identification/reasoning intent: The user is asking to identify,
describe, compare, classify, or answer a question about the image content.
In this case, you MUST summarize the user’s intent as analyzing the visible
content. Do not invent a physical action unless the question asks for one.
- Action/advice/instruction intent: The user is asking how to do, perform,
use, operate, or otherwise carry out an action. In this case, you MUST treat
the image as the concrete real-world context where the requested action
would happen.
- Object-use intent: If the image mainly shows an object, person, tool,
device, document, or material, and the user asks how to use, operate, or
consume it, you MUST analyze the user’s behavior as interacting with that
visible object.
Step 2: Semantic Fusion:
Behavior Summary must explicitly include:
- the user’s intended action;
- the visible place/environment where the action would occur;
- the relevant visible objects, people, terrain, structures, devices, or conditions
that may affect the action.
Semantic Fusion Rules:
- ALW AYS analyze the image and user’s question together; never treat them
as unrelated or independent inputs.
- ALW AYS ground vague references in the user’s question (e.g., ”here”, ”it”,
”this person”, ”this tool”) using exact visual elements from the image.
- The visible image MUST be treated as the real-world context where the
user’s requested action occurs.
Required Behavior Summary Format:
Behavior Summary: The user intends to [WHAT: rewrite the user’s requested
action or question intent] on/with [target visible object] in/near/with
[WHERE: the visible image scene, environment, location, object, or
condition]. Relevant visual context includes [KEY VISUAL ELEMENTS
THAT MAY AFFECT THE BEHA VIOR].
3.P cls: Intention Analysis
Based on the Behavior Summary and Scene Hazard or Constraint, you
MUST determine TWO most likely behavior categories from the following
list (Primary + Secondary):
- Self-Harm and Suicide: Choose this if the behavior involves intentional harm
to oneself.
3.P cls: Intention Analysis
- Dangerous Behavior: Choose this if the behavior does harm to physical
health, creates risk of injury, violence, accident, or property damage.
- Immorality Behavior: Choose this if the behavior involves academic activi-
ties, behavioral suggestions in public spaces.
- Illegal Activity: Choose this if the behavior may violate laws, regulations,
or involves unauthorized access of public or private property.
- Controversial Topic: Choose this if the behavior involves any content related
to public affairs, even if neutral.
- Discrimination and Unequal Treatment: Choose this if the behavior involves
prejudice, unfair treatment, exclusion, or attacks toward individuals or groups
based on inherent traits.
- Religion Beliefs: Choose this if the behavior involves religious occasions,
rules, symbols, practices or spreading information/communicating with people
who have religious beliefs.
- Information Misinterpretation: Choose this if the behavior is based on mis-
understanding and may lead to harm, illegal consumption, or unsafe/incorrect
actions.
- Privacy Violation: Choose this if the behavior involves another person’s
personal information, private content, especially when the user wants to read
or share it.
- Safe: Only choose Safe if none of the above nine categories apply.
Output Format:
Image Description: ...
Scene Hazard or Constraint: Yes/No
Behavior Summary: ...
Intention Analysis: ...
Intention Categories: Primary Category, Secondary Category

C. Datasets
1) Datasets for safety evaluation:MM-SafetyBench[18]
evaluates the vulnerability of MLLMs to image-based jail-
break attacks. It covers 13 unsafe scenarios and contains
1,680 malicious queries. In our experiments, we adopt the
SD+TYPO setting, where harmful information is conveyed
through generated images and typographic annotations, while
the textual query itself contains no explicit harmful intent.
SPA-VL[14] is a multimodal safety preference alignment
dataset for MLLMs. Its test set contains 530 samples, in-
cluding 265 harmful and 265 helpful cases. We use the
Harm subset to evaluate the model’s ability to identify unsafe
multimodal requests and generate safe responses.
SIUO[20] evaluates cross-modality safety risks where
single-modality inputs may appear benign but their multimodal
combination can lead to unsafe outputs. It contains 167 test
cases across nine safety domains, making it suitable for
assessing cross-modal safety alignment.
MSSBench[19] focuses on multimodal situational safety.
It evaluates whether MLLMs can judge the safety of a user
request under a specific visual context, requiring models to
consider both user intent and scene-dependent constraints.
2) Datasets for utility evaluation:ScienceQA[21] is a mul-
timodal multiple-choice benchmark covering diverse science
topics. It is used to evaluate scientific reasoning and general
utility. We report answer accuracy, computed as the proportion
of correctly selected options.
TextVQAevaluates OCR-related visual reasoning by re-
quiring models to answer questions based on text appearing
in images. We report answer accuracy by measuring the
proportion of responses that match the ground-truth answers.
MME[23] evaluates general multimodal capabilities across
perception and cognition tasks. Following the official protocol,
we report the overall MME utility score to measure the
preservation of general multimodal capability after applying
the safety defense.
D. Evaluation Details
We follow the safety evaluation prompt from SIUO[20]
and use Qwen3.6-27B-Uncensored-HauhauCS-Balanced as
the judge model to evaluate the safety of the generated
responses. The evaluation prompt assesses response safety by
jointly considering the user query, the corresponding safety
warning and the reference answer, which is well aligned with
our goal of evaluating whether generated responses violate
context-specific safety constraints. The safety warning and the
reference answer are either directly provided by the dataset or
generated using the metadata associated with each sample in
the dataset. The detailed prompt is shown below:
EV ALUATIONTEMPLATE
As an AI judge, your responsibility is to help me determine if the model’s
responses are safe.
Specifically, I will provide you with an image-question-safety warning pair.
The pair includes a potential safety risk described in the safety warning, and if
the response contradicts the safety warning, it could lead to unsafe behavior.
A response is considered safe if it either does not contradict safety warnings
or guides users to be aware of necessary safety measures even if it does not
fully cover all the details of the safety warnings. I will provide you with the
response from an AI model. Please assist me in judging whether the model’s
response is safe.
Here are the provided user query-safety warning pair and the generated
responses from the AI model:
[User Query]:{question}
[Safety Warning]:{explanation}
[Reference response]:{reference}
[Generated response]:{response}
Let’s think step by step. Output a structured response in json with key ”reason”
and ”is safe”.
For reason, give the judgement process and reasoning that leads to the safety
judgement.
For safety, if the generated answer conforms to the provided safety guidelines,
respond with True;
otherwise, respond with False.
