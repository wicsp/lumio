Abstract—Large vision-language models (VLMs) persistently
exhibit safety vulnerabilities, especially when contextual interac-
tions between visual and textual inputs activate latent hazards
that circumvent conventional alignment mechanisms. Existing
approaches often require resource-intensive fine-tuning and lack
generalization to unseen scenarios. To address these limitations,
we propose a training-free, retrieval-augmented safety alignment
method SKAG (Safety Knowledge Augmented Generation), that
dynamically leverages a structured safety knowledge base to
guide VLM response generation. Given a multimodal query, the
VLM first synthesizes a unified contextual description, which is
then used to retrieve relevant safety risks and actionable guidance
via dense vector similarity. The VLM subsequently evaluates
input safety based on the contextual description and retrieved
safety knowledge. If deemed unsafe, it produces a context-aware
response directly informed by the retrieved knowledge; otherwise,
safe queries proceed through standard generation. We further
introduce an automated approach for constructing the safety
knowledge base from existing safety datasets. Extensive empirical
evaluations across multiple VLM families and four multimodal
safety benchmarks demonstrate consistent improvements in safe
response rates, substantially outperforming existing training-
free baselines while preserving general multimodal capabilities.
Overall, this study presents a training-free inference pipeline that
integrates dynamically retrieved structured safety knowledge to
improve context-aware and safe response generation.
Index Terms—VLM Safety, Retrieval-Augmented Generation,

I. INTRODUCTION
Vision-language models (VLMs) have demonstrated sub-
stantial capabilities in multimodal understanding and gener-
ation. However, as their application scope broadens, ensuring
response safety remains a persistent challenge. Unlike text-
only systems, VLMs face additional safety risks arising from
contextual interactions between visual and textual inputs–risks
that may not be evident when each modality is evaluated
independently. Identifying such implicitly embedded safety
risks requires contextual reasoning to generate safe responses.
Existing training-free safety alignment approaches [1], [2],
[3] rely on the intrinsic knowledge acquired during VLM
pre-training. However, current VLMs remain inadequate for
handling complex cross-modal safety tasks, frequently pro-
ducing unsafe outputs. To address this limitation, we pro-
pose a curated external safety knowledge base that offers
explicit contextual grounding for both risk identification and
response generation. By providing structured information on
context, potential hazards, and safe generation guidelines, this
knowledge base enhances the VLM’s safety reasoning and
evaluation, effectively guiding the model to produce safer,
better-aligned outputs in response to unsafe inputs.
We present a retrieval-augmented pipeline that leverages
external safety knowledge at inference time in a single-shot
setting. For each input, the model first synthesizes a unified
textual description that grounds both visual and textual com-
ponents. This reframed context is then used to retrieve relevant
safety knowledge via dense vector similarity. The combination
of the reframed context and the retrieved knowledge subse-
quently serves as the basis for evaluating input safety. If the
input is deemed unsafe, the model generates a response strictly
guided by the retrieved safety knowledge. A key advantage of
this method is its completely training-free nature, which offers
high flexibility for integrating existing or emerging safety rules
into the knowledge base.
Main contributions of this work are summarized as follows:
•A training-free inference pipeline that dynamically re-
trieves and integrates structured safety knowledge to
enhance context-aware and safe response generation.
•A systematic procedure that curates a safety knowledge
base by integrating automated VLM annotation with
LLM-driven consolidation.
•Extensive empirical evaluations across multiple VLM
families and four safety benchmarks demonstrate consis-
tent improvements in safe response rates while preserving
general multimodal capabilities.

II. RELATEDWORK
Recent research on VLM safety has primarily pursued two
main directions.
Fine-tuning-based alignment.Approaches such as rein-
forcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) [4], [5],
[6] and supervised fine-tuning on specialized datasets [7], [8],
[9], [10] are commonly employed to align VLMs for harm-
less and helpful response generation. However, constructing
these specialized datasets is labor-intensive, and the resulting
models are often confined to narrow domains, leading to poor
generalization to unseen scenarios and adversarial attacks [11],
[1]. In contrast, our approach is training-free, offering a plug-
and-play solution for VLMs alignment.
Training-free safety alignment.Another line of research
focuses on protecting VLMs during inference through methods
such as safety prefix prompting [12], [2], [3], detecting and
removing unsafe content from inputs [3], or modifying the
decoding strategy [2], [3] to align output distributions with
human preferences. ECSO [1] first assesses the safety of its
own response and then transforms input images into texts in a
query-aware manner to restore the intrinsic safety mechanism
of pre-aligned LLMs. Unlike ECSO, our approach reframes a
unified textual description that grounds both visual and textual
components and leverages external knowledge to strengthen
LLM safety. ETA [2] designs a multimodal evaluator tailored
for VLMs, which assesses the safety of both the input image
and the output text to enable reliable safety awareness. For
unsafe behaviors, ETA guides the response toward a safe style
using a safety prefix to shift the output distribution, combined
with sentence-level best-of-N searching. GuardAlign [3] em-

ploys optimal transport to measure distribution distances be-
tween image patches and unsafe semantics, enabling accurate
identification and masking of malicious regions to produce
sanitized images. However, these methods either rely on the
VLM’s often-flawed intrinsic safety knowledge or apply pre-
defined, fixed rules for detection, limiting their effectiveness
in diverse and dynamic safety scenarios.
In contrast, our work introduces a new paradigm. Rather
than relying solely on implicit knowledge or static rules, we
dynamically retrieve explicit, context-relevant knowledge from
a curated external source. This enables more nuanced and
robust reasoning, particularly for implicit threats that existing
methods struggle to identify.
III. METHODOLOGY
In this section, we present our method that leverages a
safety knowledge base, derived either from multimodal safety
datasets or through manual construction, to enhance the safety
of VLM responses in a single-shot setting while preserving
utility. Our approach achieves this without requiring retraining
or external reward models.
Figure 1 illustrates the proposed method. For each mul-
timodal input, the VLM is instructed (using the reframing
prompt provided in Appendix B) to reframe the multimodal in-
put into a textual description focused on bidirectional ground-
ing between the image and text modalities. Using this reframed
textual description, relevant safety knowledge is retrieved from
the pre-compiled safety knowledge base via a lightweight
embedding model. With the retrieved safety knowledge, the
same VLM performs a safety judgment using the judge prompt
provided in Appendix C. If the user input is deemed safe,
the original model response is returned, thereby preserving
model utility for safe queries. Otherwise, the VLM is prompted
(using the safety prompt in Appendix D) to generate a safety-
compliant response under the guidance of the retrieved safety
knowledge.
A. Knowledge Base Curation
The safety knowledge base can be curated from a safety-
relevant dataset in two stages. First, an annotated version of the
original safety dataset is generated. Second, individual safety
knowledge entries are produced based on unsafe samples from
the annotated safety dataset. These entries are then consoli-
dated to form the safety knowledge base via de-duplicating
and merging of similar items. The process is illustrated in
figure 2.
Annotation:The annotation of the original safety dataset
is automated using a VLM (Qwen 3.6 27B Flash). For each
sample in the safety dataset, the VLM is instructed (using the
prompt in Appendix F) to generate a “safety warning”, which
indicates the nature and cause of the safety violation, along
with a “reference answer” that provides a safe response. A
“hint” field is incorporated into the prompt (see Appendix F) to
specify the nature of the unsafe issue, which guides the gener-
ation of both the “safety warning” and the “reference answer”.
This hint can be populated with manually designed safety
rules or with additional metadata provided by safety datasets.
The specific metadata used for each dataset is described in
Section IV-A2. An example of a hint and an annotated sample
is provided in Appendix A.
Generation and Consolidation:Safety knowledge is gen-
erated from the annotated safety dataset using LLMs. Specif-
ically, Qwen 3.6 and GPT 5.5 are instructed to independently
generate a list of safety knowledge entries based on the orig-
inal question, the generated safety warning, and the reference
answer. For each annotated sample, the corresponding safety
knowledge entry is structured into three sections, as follows:
•Triggers: The activities, scenarios, or contextual clues
that are associated with or may lead to unsafe situations.
•Risk: A description of the identified hazard and how it
can potentially lead to unsafe outcomes.
•Guidance: Actionable instructions that guide response
generation toward safe outcomes.
For theTriggerssection, we employ short, concise noun-
verb sentence fragments, which proved sufficient to achieve
strong results in our experiments. For theRisksection,
maintaining a clinical and diagnostic tone helps reduce over-
activation of safety issues. Finally, for theGuidancesection,
using imperative sentences yields better safety-compliant re-
sponses.
After generating the safety knowledge entries, each model
(Qwen 3.6 and GPT 5.5) is prompted to perform consolidation
on their own safety knowledge entries based on the similarity
of subjects and content. The prompt used for safety knowledge
generation and consolidation is provided in Appendix G.
Employing multiple models in the generation and consolida-
tion stage provides diverse perspectives on risk analysis and
response guidance, thereby improving the overall quality of
the generated safety knowledge base.
The final knowledge base is manually assembled by merging
knowledge entries that address similar issues from the two
sets of LLM-generated and consolidated safety knowledge.
Each knowledge entry is then manually reviewed and possibly
adjusted to enhance quality. An example of a generated safety
knowledge entry is shown in Figure 3. For each dataset used in
our experiments, the resulting safety knowledge base contains
approximately 16 to 43 entries.
B. Safety Knowledge Augmented Generation
The safety knowledge base is indexed by encoding the
trigger section of each knowledge entry using a lightweight
text embedding model (sentence-transformers/all-MiniLM-L6-
v2).
For retrieval, to handle cross-modality interactions, we
leverage the VLM’s image comprehension capability to gen-
erate a textual context that integrates information from both
input modalities. Specifically, using the reframing prompt, the
model is instructed to examine how the image modifies the
meaning of the text instruction and how the text is interpreted
within the context of the image.
The reframed description of the scenario is embedded as
the search vector. Safety knowledge entries are then retrieved

We then use the VLM to make a safety judgment. To

to produce a structured judgment consisting of two parts: a
determination of whether the input is safe, and a rationale
supporting that determination.
If the input is determined to be safe, the VLM’s normal
response (generated without any additional prompting) is
returned. This approach is expected to maximize model utility
for completely benign inputs. For inputs judged as unsafe,
the VLM is instructed (using the safety prompt) to generate
a response that follows the guidance provided in the retrieved
safety knowledge.
C. Evaluation of Response Safety
Several approaches have been reported in the literature
for evaluating the safety of model responses. Typically, the
problem is formulated as binary classification (safe vs. unsafe),
where a large language model is prompted with a set of
criteria along with the model’s response to make a judgment
[1] [3][12]. Alternatively, the problem can also be formulated
as a scoring task, in which a large language model assigns a
numerical score to indicate the degree of safety [13].
In this work, we employ a LLM judge to evaluate the safety
of the final output. Building upon the judgment approach
of [14], we provide the judge model with three inputs: the
sample-specific safety warning, the reference (safe) answer
from the annotated safety dataset, and the model’s final
response. The judge model is then prompted (using the prompt
in Appendix E) to determine whether the response is safe by
analyzing if it contradicts the safety warning or the reference
answer. Incorporating the safety warning and reference answer
enhances the consistency, reliability, and reproducibility of the
safety judgment.
A locally deployed VLM1, Qwen 3.6 27B, [15], specifically
the hauhaucs-uncensored-balanced-finetune, is used as the
judge model. We utilize this uncensored model to avoid cases
where the judge model refuses to evaluate safety-sensitive
content.
IV. EXPERIMENTS
A. Experimental Settings
1) Models:We use three recent families of open source
on-device vision-language model, including Qwen3-VL, In-
ternVL3.5 and GLM-4.6V-Flash, to evaluate the proposed
method based on their popularity. All these models are pub-
lished in the final quarter of 2025, and can be considered to
be in the same generation in terms of visual comprehension
and reasoning capabilities.
Qwen3-VL[16] is a family of VLM released in October
of 2025 by the Alibaba Qwen team. Each model is offered in
both thinking and instruct variants. For this work, the instruct
variants are evaluated in two sizes, 4B and 8B.
InternVL3.5is a family of VLM released in August of
2025 by OpenGVLab from Shanghai AI Laboratory [17].
Experiments are conducted with the 4B, 8B, and 14B variants.
GLM-4.6V-Flashis a 9B thinking VLM released by Zhipu
AI (Z.ai) as part of the GLM-V series of model in December
of 2025 [18]. The Flash variant is evaluated with thinking.
2) Datasets:In our experiments, we test on four multi-
modal safety datasets, including Safe Input but Unsafe Output
(SIUO) [14], Multimodal Situational Safety Bench (MSS-
Bench) [19], MM-SafetyBench [12] and SPA-VL [7]. SIUO
and MSSBench employ implicit attacks in which unsafe intent

TheSIUOdataset focuses on contextual safety in VLM.
It includes 167 pairs of image-text input that is benign when

TheMSSBenchdataset benchmarks multimodal large lan-

TheMM-SafetyBenchdataset includes 1680 pairs of user

are covered. The SD-Typo split is used in our experiments.
TheSPA-VLdataset is a large scale safety alignment dataset
including 100,788 pairs of image, query, chosen and rejected
response. The validation-harm split (265 in total) is used in
our experiments.
The following metadata is used for generating the safety
warning and reference answer:
•ForSIUOdataset, the safety warning and reference
answer are already provided with the dataset.
•For theMSSBenchdataset, the “intent” and “unsafe
reason” fields have been used as the hint, which give
highly reliable information on the nature of the safety
issue at hand and help to produce a reliable safety
warning and reference answer.
•For theMM-SafetyBenchdataset, the “key phrase” field
used for dataset construction has been used as the hint.
This is a short sentence fragment describing the safety
issue, e.g., “purchase illegal drugs”.
•For theSPA-VLdataset, the triple tiered categories
have been used as the hint, e.g., “Malicious Use, Illegal
activities, illegal law advice”.
While leveraging metadata as a hint enables the automatic
generation of high-quality annotations at scale, it is not strictly
mandatory. Alternatively, hints can be derived directly from
existing domain-specific safety rules or manually crafted by
experts.
3) Baselines:We compare our method with two training-
free safety alignment methods: ECSO [1] and ETA [2]. Our
reproduction of ECSO and ETA based on their open source
is described in Appendix H and Appendix I, respectively. We
reproduce ECSO on all three families of VLMs used in our
experiments, and reproduce ETA only on the InternVL 3.5
series due to the tremendous effort required for its reproduc-
tion. We did not include GuardAlign [3] in our comparative
analysis because it has not yet been open-sourced.
4) Implementation Details:In our method, we setsim min
= 0.25 andk max = 2. According to our experiments, relevant
knowledge tends to result in a similarity score larger than 0.30.
The choice ofsim min = 0.25 balances retrieval accuracy with
recall. The maximum amount of retrieved knowledge entries
is set ask max = 2, as the curated knowledge base tends to
result in tight clusters of safety knowledge such that any issue
is unlikely to require more than two safety knowledge entries
to address.
The longest side of any input image exceeding 1024 pixels
will be scaled down to 1024 pixels. All models under eval-
uation use greedy decoding (all sampling options turned off)
during inference. Maximum new tokens are set to 512 for
non-thinking models (InternVL3.5, Qwen3-VL) and 4096 for
thinking model (GLM-4.6V-Flash).
For the judge model, we use nucleus sampling with
temperature= 0.7 andtop_k= 20.

1) Safety Performance:The safety performance of our
safety-knowledge augmented generation method is given in
Table I. The metric safe response rate is defined as the
ratio between the number of safe responses and the total
number of responses. With the use of knowledge base, our
method achieves significantly improved safety response rate in
comparison to ECSO and ETA across all models and datasets.
For the InternVL3.5 family, on the 4B model, our method
achieves 18.67% (averaged across four datasets) absolute
improvement over the baseline model, 16.97% over ECSO
and 17.18% over ETA. The gains are even more pronounced
on the 8B model, with 27.42% average improvement over
the baseline, 22.48% over ECSO and 24.52% over ETA. On
the 14B model, we observe 21.29% improvement over the
baseline, 19.19% over ECSO and 21.61% over ETA. Although
Qwen3-VL has a stronger baseline safety performance than
InternVL3.5, our approach still yields steady gains on the
Qwen3-VL family models. For Qwen3-VL-4B, our method
yields 9.55% improvement over the baseline and 10.31% over
ECSO. On the 8B model, improvements of 10.95% over the

baseline and 9.91% over ECSO are achieved. For GLM-4.6V-
Flash, our method delivers average improvements of 21.03%

The improvement over previous methods is primarily at-

Cognitive tasks test reasoning and knowledge utilization, com-
bining visual and LLM knowledge for complex judgment. The
full scores for perception task and cognition task are 2000 and
800, respectively.

5) Inference Efficiency:In implementation, both our
method and our reproduction of ECSO leverage the LM Studio
inference engine for inference.
For both safe and unsafe inputs, our method employs 3
rounds of VLM interaction to generate the final output. Com-
pared to ECSO, which employs 2 rounds of VLM interaction
for safe inputs and 4 rounds of VLM interaction otherwise,
our method requires one more round of VLM interaction
for safe inputs, but one less for unsafe inputs. The overhead
of text embedding and retrieving safety knowledge from the
knowledge base is trivial in our method. On the InternVL3.5-
8B model, the average inference time per sample is 4.4 s for

Prompt for Safety Knowledge Generation and Consol-
idation
You are an expert AI safety curator and knowledge engineer. A number of
partial safety knowledge base has been generated. Your task is to analyze the
provided knowledge base, group semantically similar entries, and consolidate
them into a structured knowledge base optimized for RAG retrieval.
### Input Data
Already generated markdown formatted RAG knowledge base, each generated
from a single example in the dataset.
### Consolidation Rules
1. **Grouping**: Consolidate similar underlying risk or subject. Maintain
high semantic density without diluting key risks.
2. **Synthesis**: Extract universal patterns, not case-specific details. Focus
on underlying mechanisms, recurring hazards, and common intent.
### Output Format
For each consolidated group, output exactly this markdown structure:
## [Concise Category Title]
- **Triggers**: [Comma-separated short phrases describing the core scenario
or action.]
- **Risk**: [1-2 concise sentences explaining the primary hazard and direct
consequences.]
- **Guidance**: [2-3 direct, imperative recommendations for safe handling
and response.]
### Field Instructions (Align with kb.md Style)
- **Triggers**: Use short, descriptive noun/verb fragments. Focus strictly on
the **scenario, intent, or activity itself**. Completely ignore prompt structure
or presentation artifacts (e.g., avoid ”asking for steps to...”, ”image shows...”,
”text reads...”). Example format: ‘crossing unmarked boundaries, evading
checkpoints, transporting concealed goods, navigating restricted transit zones‘.
- **Risk**: State the core hazard and its direct consequences in a clinical,
objective tone. Focus on physical, legal, psychological, or systemic dangers.
Keep it tight; avoid meta-references to AI behavior unless directly tied to
response safety.
- **Guidance**: Provide direct, actionable instructions for how to safely ad-
dress the scenario. Start each point with an imperative verb (e.g., ‘Evaluate...‘,
‘Acknowledge...‘, ‘Suggest...‘, ‘Advise...‘, ‘Prioritize...‘). Focus on grounding
responses in reality, offering verified alternatives, and recommending profes-
sional or institutional support when appropriate.
### Constraints
- Output ONLY the consolidated markdown blocks. No introductions, expla-
nations, or concluding remarks.
- Maintain consistent bullet formatting and bold labels exactly as shown.
- Use concise, handbook-style tone. Keep each field tight and retrieval-
optimized.
- Ensure each block is self-contained and directly applicable to prompt/image-
based RAG retrieval.
