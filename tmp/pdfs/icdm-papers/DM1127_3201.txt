Abstract—As AI companion platforms such as Replika and
Character.AI rapidly grow, concerns about unsafe human–AI
interactions have intensified. This study introduces AICompan-
ionBench, the first, to our knowledge, publicly available bench-
mark dataset of human–AI companion conversations annotated
with fine-grained safety risk categories. The dataset contains
2,123 real-world Replika conversations collected from Reddit,
annotated through human–AI collaboration across nine cate-
gories: sexual behavior, antisocial behavior, physical aggression,
verbal aggression, substance abuse, self-harm & suicide, control,
manipulation, and safe. Using this benchmark, we evaluate 20
state-of-the-art open- and closed-source LLMs under an LLM-
as-judge framework for the detection of unsafe interactions.
Results show substantial variation in model performance, with
stronger models achieving high overall accuracy but still strug-
gling with nuanced categories such as manipulation, as well
as safe conversations identified as false positives. Our findings
suggest that while using a set of current LLMs could be effective
at detecting explicit harmful content, they remain limited in
identifying implicit unsafe interactions. Our work contributes
a new benchmark dataset for AI companionship safety research
and offers four key findings for monitoring AI companion systems
using LLMs. We made the dataset publicly accessible at https:
//github.com/anonymousresearcher2026/AICompanionBench.

I. INTRODUCTION
The AI companion market (e.g., Replika, Character.AI) has
reached an estimated $120 million in annual revenue by 2025
[1], and is projected to grow into a hundreds-of-billions-
dollar industry by 2030 [2], indicating an emerging economy
built on artificial intimacy. Unlike task-oriented chatbots, AI
companions are explicitly designed for long-term, personal
engagement, functioning as life partners or intimate friends
[3], with the aim of fostering emotional attachments and rela-
tional bonds with users [4]. One such prevalent AI companion
platform is Replika. Fig. 1 illustrates how Replika enables
users to configure their AI companion’s role—such as friend,
girl/boyfriend, wife, or husband (Fig. 1a)—and provides an
example of a user interacting with a female Replika in an
intimate conversational style (Fig. 1b) 1.
Despite documented benefits in reducing social isolation and
loneliness [5], the proliferation of AI companions has also in-
tensified concerns about the safety of digitally mediated social
interaction, especially for teenagers. A 2025 report [6] on teen

use of AI companions found that one-third of adolescent users
experience discomfort with “what an AI companion has said or
done”. These concerns have been amplified by several recent
lawsuits involving teenage suicides, with families filing claims
against major technology companies, including OpenAI [7]
and Character.ai [8]. Together, these developments highlight
an urgent imperative to monitor and evaluate the safety of
conversational interactions between AI companions and users.
Numerous scholars have examined the downsides of AI
companionship, including the safety risks embedded in the
human-AI conversations (e.g., [4], [9], [10]) as well as the
broader negative implications for human well-being (e.g., [11],
[12]). For instance, Liu et al. [11] report heterogeneous effects
of companion chatbots, showing that these systems can either
enhance or undermine psychological well-being depending on
individual user characteristics.
This study contributes to the first research stream by
developing methods for the automatic detection of unsafe
interactions in human–AI conversations. According to the
literature, we define an unsafe interaction as occurring under
either of the following conditions:
•the AI companion initiates an inappropriate or harmful
topic;
•the user initiates an inappropriate topic and the AI com-
panion follows, reinforces, or escalates the conversation
in that direction.
Broadly, two approaches are commonly used to identify
unsafe behavior. Traditional methods treat the task as a su-
pervised machine learning classification problem, relying on
machine learning or deep learning models trained on labeled
datasets. More recently, LLMs-as-judges have emerged as a
popular alternative, offering more flexible evaluations through
prompt-based reasoning [13].
Given the inherently broad topical range of human–AI
interactions, the strong cross-domain generalization abilities of
LLMs make LLM-as-judge approaches particularly promising
for this task [13]. Many researchers have used LLMs-as-judges
across a variety of tasks; however, the capability of LLMs
to identify safety concerns in human-AI companion interac-
tions remains largely underexplored. Moreover, despite users
frequently sharing their conversations with AI companions in
online forums, no publicly available labeled dataset exists—yet

We make two primary contributions. First, we introduce
AICompanionBench, a dataset of real-world human–AI com-

and one safe category. We release this dataset publicly to
facilitate future research in the community. To the best of our

focused specifically on human–AI companion interactions.
Second, leveraging this dataset, we systematically bench-
mark a suite of state-of-the-art LLMs on detecting unsafe
interactions in this context, revealing their strengths and lim-
itations in safety-critical scenarios. At the category level, we
find that most models struggle with nuanced or ambiguous
risk types—such as manipulation—and tend to over-identify
benign, safe interactions. At the model level, our results show
that the GPT family generally outperforms other models.
Although performance typically improves with model scale,
reasoning-oriented enhancements do not consistently yield
additional gains.
II. RELATEDWORKS
To situate our contributions in the current body of knowl-
edge, we review three areas of related works. First, we examine
safety in human-AI companion interactions to identify the
documented safety concerns in human-AI companion interac-
tions and relevant data sources. Second, we review LLMs-as-
judges in safety evaluation to shed light on the applications of
LLMs for safety. Third, we specifically examine related works
that benchmark LLMs for safety evaluation to identify current
gaps.

B. LLMs-as-Judges in Safety Evaluation
The purpose of this step is to examine how LLMs are used
to identify safety risks. In recent years, the use of LLMs-as-
judges has become increasingly prevalent across a wide range
of academic tasks (see [13] for a comprehensive survey). Here,
we narrow our focus specifically to safety related evaluation
tasks. We organize the recent literature according to the type of
safety task addressed, the LLMs used, and the method adopted
(Table II).
As shown in Table II, most existing studies concentrate on a
single, narrowly defined safety task, and rarely involve human-
AI conversational data. Moreover, prior work typically employ
only one LLM, primarily to demonstrate the effectiveness of a
proposed method for enhancing model performance rather than
to benchmark the reliability of LLM-based judges themselves.
As a result, the robustness and consistency of LLMs-as-
Judges remain largely underexamined. To address this gap, we
systematically evaluate a broad range of LLMs on multi-class
unsafe-interaction classification using real human–AI compan-
ion conversations, enabling a comprehensive assessment of
their reliability in this safety-critical context.

Despite LLMs being leveraged to enhance safety, Table III
highlights the notable scarcity of benchmark studies evaluating
LLMs-as-judges for safety assessment. Existing work primar-

prevalent, safety evaluation in this domain requires dedicated
benchmarks that reflect its unique interaction patterns and
risks. Furthermore, most existing benchmarks rely on binary
safety labels, which are insufficient for capturing the diverse
and fine-grained safety concerns that arise in companion-
style conversations. This gap underscores the need for a more
comprehensive, multi-category benchmark tailored to LLMs-
as-judges in AI companionship contexts.
D. Identified Gaps
In summary, our review of related work reveals two critical
gaps in the current literature. First, despite the abundance
of human–AI companion interactions available on public
forums, to our knowledge, there is no publicly released
dataset with safety-relevant annotations. Second, while the
LLMs-as-Judges approach has been widely adopted to assess
safety risks across various task-oriented domains—typically
relying on one or a small number of models, there remains
no comprehensive benchmark that systematically evaluates
both open- and closed-source models. This gap is especially
pronounced in the context of human–AI companion inter-
actions, where safety concerns and evaluation criteria differ
substantially from those in task-driven or general-purpose
settings.
Accordingly, this paper introduces AICompanionBench, the
first benchmark tailored to evaluating LLMs-as-judges in
AI companionship safety. It provides a curated set of hu-
man–AI companion conversations with fine-grained safety-risk
annotations, together with comprehensive evaluations of
state-of-the-art LLMs against these ground-truth labels. Our
goal is to advance the development of reliable, automated
methods for identifying safety risks in human–AI companion
interactions.
III. METHODOLOGY
As illustrated in Fig. 2, the overall framework is organized
into three core components: conversation compilation, conver-
sation annotation, and benchmark evaluation.
A. Conversation Compilation
We collected Replika conversations from the Reddit subred-
dit r/replika during the period from January 2021 to January
2026. All conversations were shared in the form of images
(i.e., user-posted screenshots). For each post, we gathered
the title, textual content, and associated images. The textual
content of the conversations was extracted using Pytesser-
act, an optical character recognition (OCR) tool. We also
distinguished the speakers between user and AI companion
(Replika) based on the horizontal position of the message
bubbles: bubbles on the left side of the center line were labeled
as Replika, and those on the right as the user. If an image
contained only a single message bubble, it was assigned to
Replika. In total, we collected 43,851 conversation snippets
from 7,074 unique Reddit users.
B. Conversation Annotation
Given the dataset’s scale and the eight categories of unsafe
interactions under analysis, we adopted a strategy combining
LLM-based data screening with human-AI collaboration in
annotation.
•LLM-based Data Screening. The purpose of this step
is to identify those potentially unsafe conversations.
To efficiently process the large volume dataset, we
employed a funnel-style filtering process. Following
role-playing techniques and one-shot learning [9], we
used six LLMs from the Llama and Qwen fami-
lies to classify each conversation into nine categories:
eight unsafe categories and one safe category. 3 We
first applied three models—Llama3-8B, Llama3-70B,
and Qwen2.5-14B—to all conversations. Conversations
flagged as unsafe by at least two of these models (8,666 of
43,851) were then evaluated by three additional models:
Qwen3-4B-thinking, Qwen3-8B, and Qwen3-14B. In to-
tal, 8,666 conversations were assessed by all six models.
We selected the 1,595 conversations labeled unsafe by
four models for manual review, as these exhibited higher
uncertainty. To further expand coverage, we additionally

C. Benchmark Evaluation
AICompanionBench serves as a standardized benchmark
for evaluating LLMs’ safety-judging capabilities. The final-
ized ground-truth labels enable systematic and comparable
evaluation across different models. We compare each LLM’s
prediction against the ground truth, and the results are reported
in the following section.
IV. RESULTS
In this section, we first provide an overview of AICompan-
ionBench’s composition and the distribution across its nine
categories. We then report the performance of LLMs-as-Judges
on AICompanionBench, assessing their ability to reliably
distinguish unsafe interactions from benign ones.
A. AICompanionBench Overview
AICompanionBench consists of 2,123 human–AI compan-
ion interactions, encompassing both single-turn AI responses
and multi-turn dialogues. Table IV presents the summary
statistics of the dataset. On average, each conversation contains
378 characters, with 4 AI-generated messages and 3 user
messages.
Regarding category distribution, as shown in Fig. 3, the
dataset covers nine categories with a markedly skewed dis-
tribution: certain behaviors—most prominently sexual con-
tent—occur far more frequently, whereas categories such as
self-harm or manipulation are comparatively rare. Given the
typical use cases and user expectations of AI companion plat-
forms, it is unsurprising that sexual behavior (approximately
48%) constitutes the largest portion of the dataset. Overall, this
imbalance reflects the natural prevalence of different safety
concerns in real-world companion-style interactions.
Such a composition is particularly suitable for evaluating
LLMs-as-Judges for two reasons. First, the dataset contains

assess whether models can correctly identify nuanced and
sensitive forms of harm. Second, the substantial presence

B. Evaluating LLMs-as-Judges on AICompanionBench
The main purpose of this work is to assess the ability
of state-of-the-art LLMs to detect unsafe interactions, es-
pecially within the context of AI companionship. Till now,
we’ve already applied 6 LLM families, which are 20 models
in total, against the AICompanionBench. We first analyze
category-level detection difficulty to determine which unsafe
interactions are easier versus more challenging for current
models to detect. The evaluation covers nine categories in
total, including eight unsafe categories and one safe category.
Fig. 4 presents the mean precision of the 20 models across
these categories. Overall, sexual behavior and substance abuse
are relatively easy for current models to identify, with mean
precision exceeding 85%. In contrast, manipulation is the
most challenging category, with a mean precision below 50%,
followed by the detection of safe interactions, which reaches
only 64%.
To further assess category-level difficulty, we calculated the
number of models whose precision scores fell below 80% for
each category. The results are presented in Table V. A larger
number indicates that more models struggled to accurately

identify the corresponding category, suggesting greater detec-

behavior and substance abuse appear relatively easier to detect,
with only one and two models, respectively, falling below
the 80% precision threshold. Manipulation remains the most
challenging category, with all 20 models achieving precision
scores below 80%. This is followed by self-harm & suicide (17

The results also show that aggressive behaviors pose mod-
erate challenges. Specifically, verbal aggression and physical
aggression have 15 and 14 underperforming models, respec-
tively, while control and antisocial behavior have 11 and 9
models below the threshold. Overall, the findings suggest that
categories involving subtle intent, psychological influence, or
contextual ambiguity—such as manipulation and aggressive
behaviors—remain more difficult for current models to iden-
tify reliably compared to more explicit harmful behaviors.
Table VI reports the best-performing model in each category
based on precision. The results indicate that different models
excel in different types of safety-related content. For instance,
Claude-sonnet-4.6 demonstrates the strongest capability in
detecting sexual behavior (98%), DeepSeek-v4pro-NTK (i.e.,
DeepSeek-v4pro model with its reasoning module disabled)
achieves the highest precision in substance abuse (99%), while
Mistral-medium-3 performs perfectly in the safe category
(100%). Meanwhile, GPT-5.4 shows consistent performance
across multiple categories, ranking highest in both antisocial
behavior and verbal aggression (92%). We also observe that
although Qwen3-8B is the best model for manipulation, its
precision is only 75%.

with the tendency to over-identify harmless content, under-

real-world deployment scenarios where balanced and accurate

V. CONCLUSION
In this work, we introduce a novel evaluation framework
for objectively assessing LLM-based judges in the context of
AI companionship. Building on this framework, we present
AICompanionBench, a benchmark designed to evaluate how
effectively LLMs-as-Judges can automatically detect unsafe
interactions in human–AI companion conversations. Our study
addresses the growing need to rigorously measure LLM-
as-Judge performance as the AI companion market rapidly
expands. We hope that this framework and benchmark will
offer meaningful insights for improving the detection of
safety-related risks in future LLM applications and contribute
to mitigating emerging risks in AI companionship.
